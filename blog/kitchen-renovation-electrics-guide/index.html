Kitchen Renovation Electrics: What to Plan Before Your Fitter Arrives

7 July 2026 · 6 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Sockets must sit at least 300mm from a sink and at least 450mm above the floor under UK regulations
  • Ovens, hobs, and large appliances usually need their own dedicated circuit, not a shared socket circuit
  • All new kitchen wiring must be RCD protected
  • Getting the layout agreed before units go in avoids expensive retrofitting later

A kitchen carries more electrical load, in a smaller space, than any other room in the house — which is exactly why it's the room most likely to go wrong if the electrics aren't planned before the units arrive.

Socket placement rules

There are clear regulations behind where kitchen sockets can go, not just design preference: sockets need to sit at least 300mm (horizontal distance) from the edge of a sink to reduce water risk, and at least 450mm above the floor under UK building regulations. A common practical placement is around 150mm above the worktop, or 100mm above an upstand — high enough to be usable, low enough not to look like an afterthought.

Circuits: what needs its own

General kitchen worktop sockets usually run off a ring final circuit or a 20A radial circuit. But bigger appliances typically need their own dedicated circuit rather than sharing: an electric oven or hob commonly needs a 32A or 40A radial circuit of its own, and it's good practice to give a dishwasher, washing machine, and any American-style fridge-freezer their own circuits too, rather than loading them onto the general sockets.

RCD protection is non-negotiable

Under current building regulations, all new kitchen wiring must be RCD protected — this isn't a nice-to-have given how much water and how many appliances share the space, it's a requirement, and it should be built into your consumer unit setup rather than treated as optional.

Timing with your kitchen fitter

The order of operations matters: electrics need to be agreed and largely first-fixed before your kitchen units go in, not worked around afterwards. That means the socket layout, lighting positions, and any dedicated circuits should be settled with your electrician while you're still finalising the kitchen design — ideally with your fitter or designer in the conversation too, so nobody's plans conflict once work starts.

Don't forget the lighting

Under-cabinet and feature lighting is easy to leave as an afterthought and hard to add cleanly once units are fitted. If you want it, it needs wiring in at first fix, with cable routed to sit invisibly behind cabinetry rather than run externally after the fact.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I book an electrician relative to my kitchen fitter?
Before the units go in, ideally at first-fix stage, so sockets and circuits land in the right places once cabinets are fitted.
Do induction hobs need a dedicated circuit?
Yes, almost always — we size the circuit to the specific hob being installed.
Can you work directly with my kitchen designer or fitter?
Yes, we regularly work from kitchen design plans and coordinate directly with the fitter.
How long does a kitchen electrical first and second fix take?
Typically 1–2 days combined, depending on the size of the kitchen and how much lighting is involved.
